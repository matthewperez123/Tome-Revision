# Tome Art Factory Style Calibration - 2026-06-16

## Current Update - 2026-06-18

This calibration has been revised back toward the Meridian white-marble
underlay direction for Tome app front-cover production:

- `docs/art-factory/meridian-signal-cover-template.md`

Use Meridian Signal / white-marble minimalism as the current default for
generated Tome covers. Keep the covers flat, restrained, quiet, textless, and
book-specific without becoming photorealistic, painterly, dramatic, or
detail-heavy.

The current cover target is a 2:3 mobile artwork on a warm white marble /
stone-paper underlay with a larger, lighter, subtler palette. Use broad color
fields, one simple symbolic vignette, and generous quiet space. Do not add
extra figures, props, narrative moments, dramatic lighting, or rendered detail.
The Color-Flood direction is now archived guidance only unless a prompt
explicitly asks for it.

## Current 2026-06-16 House Style

The following notes document the Meridian Signal / white-underlay direction.
They are current again for palette, safety, and app-readability.

At this stage, the Art Deco exploration, character-heavy scene explorations, and
brighter cubist detours were superseded by Meridian Signal Covers: a sharper
branch of Quiet Scenic Classics that borrows the repeatable white-underlay
discipline of recent Meridian Classics while keeping Tome covers textless,
app-readable, scenery-first, symbolic, and safe for a school/classroom context.

Chronology read:

- The repo cover archive and `public/art-factory/reference/meridian/meridian-sample-sheet.jpg`
  represent the older cream Meridian production line.
- The latest saved cover-like local files found in Downloads are dated
  2025-05-27 to 2025-05-28 and still show cream typography-heavy fronts.
- The 2026 ChatGPT cover history evolves toward palette-locking and material
  language: neutral paper fields, exact palette reuse, no purple drift, no
  reinterpretation, and front/back consistency.
- The user's current correction points beyond saved cream covers, recent
  character posters, and painterly/cubist tests: use some color, less detail,
  more minimalism, mostly symbolism and innuendo, no vulgar or grotesque
  imagery, and a greater emphasis on scenery over people.

Core traits:

- Flat contemporary editorial illustration with matte shapes.
- White-stone / warm ivory underlay with very subtle texture.
- Strong negative space and thumbnail readability for the Tome app.
- Quiet scenic specificity: one recognizable landscape, horizon, building,
  gate, window, road, sea, mountain, or architectural threshold.
- One symbolic clue per cover: key, ribbon, lantern, lily, rose, sail, rope,
  empty chair, covered frame, window glow, red signal, or similar.
- Penguin/Oxford-like subject discipline: pick canonical motifs from the work,
  its period, and its scholarly reception without copying brand trade dress.
- People and characters should be absent or tiny. Use them only as scale marks
  when needed; no detailed faces, dramatic bodies, or character-led scenes.
- Wide Stoa backgrounds with left-weighted symbolic objects and broad UI-safe space.
- Disciplined bright accents: cobalt, emerald, coral, saffron, teal, vermilion,
  chartreuse, and sky blue on a mostly white stone-paper base.
- No readable text, no photorealism, no 3D, no Art Deco, no old cream layout,
  no ornate frames, no fake typography, no beige drift, no purple cast, no
  vulgarity, no grotesque imagery, no gore, no monsters.

## Meridian Signal Covers Template Read

`35-meridian-signal-covers-white-underlay-v2.png` is the clearest aesthetic
proof for the Meridian Signal branch. It is closer to the most recent Meridian
white-underlay covers than the more painterly `34` proof: flatter, more
symbolic, more negative space, and less dependent on scenic detail. It keeps
the white/ivory title-safe field from the recent Meridian cover archive and
turns the lower art into a single symbolic vignette or simple image panel.

Use this dedicated template:

- `docs/art-factory/meridian-signal-cover-template.md`

Template rules:

- Full cover: warm ivory / white-stone underlay.
- Top 28-35%: mostly blank title-safe field.
- Middle/lower area: one flat symbolic vignette or simple lower panel.
- Optional footer: muted color slab with no text.
- 3-5 broad scenic elements only.
- One setting signal, one symbolic object, one light cue.
- Some color, but disciplined: muted teal, sky blue, pine green, pale gold,
  terracotta/coral, sage, dusk navy, warm ivory.
- Symbolism should feel suggestive rather than literal: a key near a gate, a
  red ribbon on an empty chair, a white lily by dark water, a rope on a deck,
  a lit upper window, a single red signal.
- People are absent by default and tiny when needed.
- No readable text, fake title, fake author, logos, ornate borders, crowded
  props, cubism, Art Deco, photorealism, 3D, gore, grotesque imagery, monsters,
  skulls, or vulgarity.

## Quiet Scenic Classics Read

`25-quiet-scenic-classics-cover-tests-v2.png` is the best current direction.
It corrects the prior drift by reducing detail, minimizing or removing people,
and making each cover feel like a real modern classics cover rather than a
busy illustrated scene. The strongest traits are scenery-first composition,
title-safe blank zones, pale underlays, 3-5 large color planes, and one
book-specific symbolic clue.

Prompts should emphasize:

- "Quiet Scenic Classics"
- "scenery first, people absent or tiny"
- "one landscape, horizon, gate, window, road, sea, mountain, or architectural threshold"
- "3-5 large flat shapes"
- "pale paper / marble-white / warm ivory underlay"
- "title-safe blank upper third"
- "one symbolic clue and one warm accent"
- "no vulgarity, no grotesque imagery, no gore, no monsters"
- "no detailed faces, no dramatic human bodies"
- "textless Tome app cover art"

- Macbeth: empty heath, distant castle, red moon, prophecy branch.
- Frankenstein: Alpine ice, distant warm laboratory window, one lightning line.
- Jane Eyre: moor, Thornfield silhouette, warm upper window, wind-shaped tree.
- The Iliad: Trojan plain, city wall, ships, tents, bronze shield, dawn smoke.
- The Odyssey: wide sea, mast, island horizon, gold star path.
- Moby-Dick: vast ocean, white whale-shaped wave, tiny boat, restrained harpoon line.
- Pride and Prejudice: Pemberley garden path, estate silhouette, tiny distant scale figures.
- The Divine Comedy: terraced mountain, gate, ascending color bands, stars.

## Archived Modern Marble Redo Read

`23-classical-scene-cubist-cover-tests-v2.png` was the best prior direction.
It improves on the earlier marble sheets by using less detail, more color,
larger figures, and more direct scenery. It feels closer to classical painting
subject matter in the Penguin/Oxford sense, but compressed into simplified
cubist/collage planes for small app visibility. It is now useful mainly as a
comparison point because it remains too character-led and scene-dense for the
Quiet Scenic Classics direction.

Prompts should emphasize:

- "white marble / uninked archival paper underlay"
- "modern minimalist classical-scene collage"
- "stylized people and characters in a famous scene"
- "less detail, more color, fewer tiny motifs"
- "cubist compression of foreground, midground, and background"
- "textless Tome app cover art"
- "bright disciplined accent palette"
- "no Art Deco, no old cream Meridian typography, no photorealism"

## Archived Art Deco Read

The Divine Comedy is the strongest proof of the locked system. The vertical
three-realm ascent gives it the canonical seriousness the app needs, while the
geometry keeps it modern.

Macbeth remains the cleanest Shakespeare direction. The cover should favor
theatrical architecture and symbolic stage objects over skulls, actors, or
witches.

Frankenstein works best as science, mountain, lightning, and fragile creation.
Avoid monster iconography entirely.

The Count of Monte Cristo gives the strongest adventure-canon structure:
prison window, sea, chain, sealed letter, jewel.

The Great Gatsby can use Art Deco naturally, but must avoid the famous
face/eyes cover vocabulary and party-glamour cliche.

Jane Eyre has the right emotional temperature, but future variants should
simplify the foliage and make the window/key/tree system clearer.

## Refinement Notes

- Pride and Prejudice should become more geometric and less decorative.
- Frankenstein should avoid horror poster cues and stay quiet, symbolic, and app-native.
- The Divine Comedy works best when the three realms are specific but simplified.
- Hamlet should reduce the skull scale and lean more stage/ghost/castle.
- Treasure Island should avoid prop density and use cleaner map/island hierarchy.
- Stoa prompts should always specify the quiet UI zone location.
- Covers should stay to one dominant poster scene with 3-5 major motifs.
